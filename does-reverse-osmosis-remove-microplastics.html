Does Reverse Osmosis Remove Microplastics? (Yes — Here's What the Science Says)

Yes — reverse osmosis removes microplastics, and it’s one of the most effective home methods for doing so. The RO membrane’s pores are thousands of times smaller than even the tiniest microplastic particles, so it physically blocks essentially all of them — typically 99%+ reduction, including the much smaller nanoplastics that slip through ordinary filters. This guide explains how RO removes microplastics, what recent science actually found, and why the pitcher filter on your counter can’t do the same job.

The Short Answer

Reverse osmosis is among the best home defenses against microplastics. Here’s the scale that makes it work: the RO membrane has pores around 0.0001 microns (0.1 nanometers), while microplastics are defined as plastic particles smaller than 5 millimeters, and the ones found in drinking water are typically 1 micron or larger. That’s a size gap of roughly 10,000-to-1 — the particles simply can’t fit through the membrane and are flushed away as wastewater.

Even nanoplastics — the much smaller fragments (under 1 micron) that recent research has detected — are largely rejected by RO, because the membrane operates at the molecular scale. No home filter is perfect, but reverse osmosis is as close as residential water treatment gets for plastic particles.

How Reverse Osmosis Removes Microplastics

Reverse osmosis works by forcing water under pressure through a semipermeable membrane so fine it rejects dissolved ions — things far smaller than any plastic particle. (For the full mechanism, see What Is Reverse Osmosis Water?.) Because microplastics and even nanoplastics are vastly larger than the membrane’s pore size, they’re physically blocked. There’s no chemistry or adsorption involved — it’s a straightforward size barrier, which is why RO is so reliable for particles.

This is fundamentally different from how a carbon filter works. Carbon removes contaminants by adsorption (molecules sticking to the carbon surface), which is great for chlorine and taste but doesn’t reliably catch plastic particles. A membrane that physically strains the water is the right tool for the job.

What the Science Actually Found

Microplastics in drinking water moved from fringe worry to mainstream concern over the past few years, and the data is worth understanding:

  • Microplastics have been detected in the majority of tap and bottled water samples tested worldwide.
  • A widely-reported 2024 study using advanced imaging found that a typical liter of bottled water contained around 240,000 plastic particles — far more than earlier estimates, and most of them in the nanoplastic size range that older methods couldn’t even see.
  • The health effects of ingesting micro- and nanoplastics are still being researched and aren’t fully understood, but the precautionary interest in reducing exposure is reasonable and widespread.

The practical takeaway: bottled water is not a reliable escape from plastics (it may be worse), and most home filters don’t remove them. Reverse osmosis is one of the few approaches that addresses both microplastics and the smaller nanoplastics, because it filters at the molecular scale.

Which Filters Remove Microplastics — and Which Don’t

If reducing plastic particles is a priority, the filtration type matters more than the brand:

  • Reverse osmosis (best): physically blocks micro- and nanoplastics. Any credible RO system does this — see our best reverse osmosis systems guide or, for renters, the countertop options.
  • Distillation: very effective, but impractical for most households’ daily volume.
  • Tight carbon block filters: can capture larger microplastics, but won’t reliably stop nanoplastics.
  • Standard pitcher and faucet filters: built for chlorine and taste, not particle removal — don’t rely on them for plastics.
  • Refrigerator filters: same limitation as pitcher filters.

For a system that addresses microplastics along with lead, fluoride, PFAS, and the rest in one unit, reverse osmosis is the practical answer.

Can You Test Your Water for Microplastics at Home?

Honestly, not really — and it’s worth being straight about this. Detecting and counting microplastics (let alone nanoplastics) requires specialized lab equipment like spectroscopy; the home water test strips and kits that measure things like lead, hardness, and chlorine cannot measure plastic particles. A few specialty labs offer microplastic analysis as a paid mail-in service, but it’s expensive and not something most homeowners need.

The practical move isn’t to test for plastics — it’s to filter them out preemptively with reverse osmosis, since the method works regardless of how many particles your specific water contains. You can confirm your RO system is healthy overall with a simple TDS meter (it verifies the membrane is reducing dissolved solids), even though TDS doesn’t measure plastics directly.

Where Microplastics in Water Come From

Understanding the sources helps explain why they’re so hard to avoid without filtration:

  • Plastic packaging and bottles. Bottled water picks up particles from the bottle and cap itself — part of why bottled water tests so high.
  • Degraded plastic waste. Larger plastic litter breaks down in the environment into ever-smaller fragments that reach water supplies.
  • Water distribution pipes. Some particles shed from plastic piping and fittings between the treatment plant and your tap.
  • Synthetic textiles. Washing machines release microfibers from polyester and nylon clothing into wastewater, some of which cycles back.
  • Industrial and consumer products. Microbeads (now banned in many products), tire wear, and manufacturing all contribute.

Because the sources are everywhere, both municipal and well water can carry microplastics. Conventional municipal treatment removes many larger particles but isn’t designed to catch the smallest ones — which is why point-of-use reverse osmosis is the dependable backstop.

FAQ

Does reverse osmosis remove nanoplastics too?

Largely, yes. Nanoplastics (particles under 1 micron) are smaller than microplastics but still vastly larger than the RO membrane’s ~0.0001-micron pores, so the membrane rejects the great majority of them. RO is one of the only home methods that addresses nanoplastics at all — most filters don’t.

Does a Brita or pitcher filter remove microplastics?

Not reliably. Standard pitcher, faucet, and refrigerator filters use activated carbon designed for chlorine and taste, not particle filtration. They may capture some larger particles incidentally, but they’re not a dependable barrier against microplastics and don’t stop nanoplastics. Reverse osmosis is the practical home method.

Is bottled water free of microplastics?

No — and it may be worse than tap. A 2024 study found a typical liter of bottled water contained roughly 240,000 plastic particles, most of them nanoplastics. Switching to bottled water is not a reliable way to avoid plastics; filtering your own water with RO is more effective and far cheaper over time.

Does boiling water remove microplastics?

Not reliably. Some research suggests boiling very hard water can trap a portion of particles inside the limescale that forms, but it’s inconsistent and doesn’t work for soft water. For dependable removal, you need reverse osmosis or distillation, not boiling.

How do I know my RO system is removing microplastics?

You can’t measure plastics at home, but you don’t need to — RO removes them as a function of its pore size, so a properly working system handles them automatically. Confirm the system is healthy overall with a TDS meter (it shows the membrane is reducing dissolved solids) and keep up with filter changes.

Do I need a special filter for microplastics, or does a normal RO system work?

A normal reverse osmosis system already removes microplastics — you don’t need a special add-on. The standard RO membrane is the part that does the work. Just choose any credible RO system from our best RO guide and keep it maintained.

Are there microplastics in tap water?

Yes — studies have detected microplastics in the majority of tap water samples worldwide, though typically at far lower levels than bottled water. The exact amount varies by location and source. Reverse osmosis removes them regardless of how many your specific water contains.

Are microplastics in drinking water harmful?

The health effects of ingesting micro- and nanoplastics are still being actively researched and aren’t fully established. There’s reasonable precautionary interest in reducing exposure, which is why many people choose to filter them out — but current science hasn’t quantified the risk. We stick to what’s known: RO removes them effectively if you want to reduce your exposure.

Bottom Line

Reverse osmosis removes microplastics — 99%+, including most of the smaller nanoplastics that recent science has detected in tap and bottled water — because its membrane filters at a scale thousands of times finer than any plastic particle. Pitcher filters, fridge filters, and bottled water don’t offer the same protection. If reducing your exposure to plastic particles is a goal, a standard RO system handles it automatically, along with a long list of other contaminants.
